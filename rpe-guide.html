RPE Guide: Rating of Perceived Exertion

Learn how to accurately rate your effort and use RPE for better training

What is RPE?

RPE (Rating of Perceived Exertion) is a subjective scale used to measure how hard an exercise feels to you. It helps you train at the right intensity and track progress more effectively than just counting reps and weight.

Why RPE Matters

  • Autoregulation: Adjust training based on how you feel each day
  • Progress tracking: Compare efforts across different weights and exercises
  • Injury prevention: Avoid pushing too hard when fatigued
  • Consistency: Train at prescribed intensities regardless of external factors

The RPE Scale (1-10)

RPE Effort Level Reps Left Description When to Use
1-3 Very Easy 10+ Barely any effort. Could do many more reps easily. Warm-up, recovery
4-5 Light 6-8 Still comfortable and controlled. Technique work, easy days
6 Moderate 4 Getting challenging but sustainable. Volume work, hypertrophy
7 Hard 3 Definitely working hard, good effort. Most strength training
8 Very Hard 2 Really pushing it, form still good. Heavy sets, intensity work
9 Extremely Hard 1 Maximum sustainable effort. Peak weeks, testing (sparingly)

How to Use RPE

During Your Set:

  1. Focus on the last 2-3 reps - These matter most for RPE rating
  2. Consider form quality - If form breaks down, you're likely at RPE 8+
  3. Think "reps in reserve" - How many more good reps could you do?
  4. Rate immediately after - Don't wait; your perception changes quickly

Between Sets:

  • Adjust weight if needed: If RPE was too low/high, modify next set
  • Rest appropriately: Higher RPE sets need more rest
  • Track honestly: Record what you actually felt, not what you "should" feel

Practical Example: Goblet Squats

Scenario: 3 sets of 8 reps @ RPE 7

Set 1: 35 lb × 8 reps

How it felt: Last 2 reps were challenging but controlled. Could definitely do 3 more with good form.

RPE Rating: 7 ✓ (Perfect - matches target)

Set 2: 35 lb × 8 reps

How it felt: A bit more fatigued. Last rep was quite hard, maybe 1-2 more reps left.

RPE Rating: 8 (Slightly high - stay at this weight for set 3)

Set 3: 35 lb × 8 reps

How it felt: Really had to focus on the last 3 reps. Could maybe squeeze out 2 more.

RPE Rating: 8 (Appropriate progression - ready for more weight next week)

Key Takeaways:

  • Weight was appropriate - hit target RPE on first set
  • Natural fatigue progression (7→8→8) is normal and healthy
  • Ready to progress to 40 lb next session since we ended at RPE 8
  • Good form maintained throughout = accurate RPE ratings

Common RPE Mistakes

❌ "Sandbagging"

Rating everything 5-6 when you could actually do more. Limits progress.

Fix: Push yourself to feel what RPE 7-8 actually is.

❌ "Hero Mode"

Always rating 9-10, going to failure every set. Leads to burnout.

Fix: Practice stopping at RPE 7-8 most of the time.

❌ "Overthinking"

Spending too much time debating between RPE 7 vs 7.5. Paralysis by analysis.

Fix: Go with your gut. Close enough is good enough.

❌ "External Focus"

Rating based on weight lifted rather than how it actually felt.

Fix: Focus on internal sensations, not the numbers.

Quick Reference

RPE by Reps in Reserve

  • RPE 6 = 4+ reps left
  • RPE 7 = 3 reps left
  • RPE 8 = 2 reps left
  • RPE 9 = 1 rep left
  • RPE 10 = 0 reps left (failure)

When to Use Each RPE

  • RPE 6-7: Volume work, technique practice
  • RPE 7-8: Most strength training
  • RPE 8-9: Peak/test weeks, advanced athletes
  • RPE 9-10: Rarely needed, use with caution

Remember: RPE is a Skill

Like any skill, using RPE accurately takes practice. Start by focusing on the difference between RPE 7 (could do 3 more) and RPE 8 (could do 2 more). With time, you'll develop better internal awareness and more consistent ratings.

Trust the process - even experienced lifters sometimes miss their RPE by 0.5-1 point. The goal is consistency and honesty, not perfection.
